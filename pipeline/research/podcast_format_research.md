## 1. The Daily (NYT) — Format Deep Dive

### Episode Structure

The Daily runs ~20 minutes, five days a week. Each episode focuses on **one story** — a deliberate constraint that forces depth over breadth. The episode architecture follows a clear three-act structure:

| Section | Timing | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Cold Hook / Teaser** | 0:00–0:45 | A provocative statement or sound clip that lands you mid-story. No "welcome back" preamble. |
| **Act 1: The Explainer** | 0:45–6:00 | Barbaro interviews the NYT reporter who broke the story. They lay out what happened, the key characters, the stakes. Feels like a friend briefing you. |
| **Act 2: The Complication** | 6:00–14:00 | The "so why does this matter?" pivot. The deeper context — historical, political, human. This is where archival tape, field sound, and secondary voices come in. |
| **Act 3: The Resolution / So What** | 14:00–19:00 | What happens next? Open questions are OK — The Daily does not force false closure. |
| **Outro** | 19:00–20:00 | Brief. "I'm Michael Barbaro. See you tomorrow." Often no music swell — it just ends. |

**Key structural rule:** The story always has **a character at the center**, not a concept. The Venezuelan economy becomes one Venezuelan family. The Iran nuclear deal becomes one diplomat's dilemma.

### Michael Barbaro's Interview Technique

The New Yorker described Barbaro's voice as "a twenty-minute update murmured in your ear by a well-informed, sensitive, funny, modest friend — the kind of person who has as many questions as answers."

What he actually does differently:

1. **The productive pause.** After a reporter finishes explaining something, Barbaro sits in silence for 1–2 seconds longer than comfortable. This prompts the reporter to add the unguarded detail — the thing they weren't going to say. Listeners feel like they're in the room.

2. **"So what you're saying is..."** He actively restates in simpler terms. This serves two functions: it confirms understanding and it signals to the listener "here's the key takeaway." It's a compression technique disguised as empathy.

3. **He records with a Google Doc open.** Mid-interview, Barbaro and producers are rewriting questions in real time on a shared doc. If the story is going in an unexpected direction, they adjust. The final episode is partly written after it's recorded.

4. **He models the listener's emotional response.** When a reporter reveals something shocking, Barbaro says "Wow" or "That's extraordinary." These reactions give the listener permission to feel something. It's not performance — it's emotional signposting.

5. **Transparent journalism.** He asks "How do you know this?" and "Walk me through how you reported this." This radical transparency about process rebuilds trust in an era of media skepticism.

**Sample Barbaro line structure:**
> *"Let me just make sure I understand what you're saying. You're telling me that [simplified restatement]. And the significance of that is [emotional implication]."*

### Why The Daily Achieves ~70% Retention

The industry average episode completion rate is approximately 40–50%. The Daily consistently performs in the 65–75% range. Key reasons:

1. **One story = no decision fatigue.** Listeners never have to decide whether to stay for the next segment. There's only one segment.

2. **The "walk with the reporter" format.** You don't hear the polished finished story — you hear someone explaining it to someone else discovering it. The discovery is the product.

3. **Authentic imperfection.** Reporters sometimes stumble, correct themselves, laugh. This signals the conversation is real and not pre-written.

4. **20-minute length is disciplined.** Per Spotify analytics (2024), podcasts in the 20–40 minute range achieve a median 74% consumption rate. The Daily's 20-minute target fits this sweet spot.

5. **Habitual positioning.** Released at 6 AM. Engineered for the morning commute. Listeners build a ritual — and ritual beats content quality for long-term retention.

### Music & Sound Design

- **The Daily's signature theme** is minimalist, slightly melancholy piano — composed specifically to feel neither triumphant nor anxious. It signals "serious but not alarming."
- **Field audio** is central: crowd sounds, phone calls, press conference snippets. This is not decoration — it's evidence. Listeners "hear the world."
- **Music beds** are used sparingly and play at approximately -20 dB beneath narration during emotional moments.
- **Transitions** between acts use a brief 2–3 second music sting — the same sound every time. Listeners learn the audio grammar: this sound means "we're going deeper now."
- **Silence** is used deliberately. A 2-second gap before a major revelation hits harder than any musical flourish.

## 2. NPR Up First / Planet Money — Short-Form Mastery

### Up First: The 10-Minute Formula

Up First launched in April 2017, born from listener data on NPR One showing sharp drop-offs when newscasts exceeded 5 minutes. NPR decided: don't fight listener behavior, design around it.

**Format:**
- **3 stories** in ~10–15 minutes
- Released at **6:00–6:30 AM ET** weekdays
- Multiple rotating hosts — familiarity over any single personality
- Each story gets ~3–4 minutes: a setup + a field clip + "what comes next"

**The cold open technique:** Up First does not open with "Good morning, I'm [name], and today in the news..." It opens with the **most important fact** of the most important story. Example:

> *"A ceasefire has collapsed in Gaza. Here's what happened overnight." — [tape rolls immediately]*

The host introduction comes **after** the first clip, not before. This is exactly backwards from traditional broadcast news. The listener is hooked before they know who's talking.

**Key NPR data:** In internal NPR One listening pattern analysis, stories with introductions under 22 seconds dramatically outperformed longer intros. The worst-performing intros started with self-reference ("In the second of our three-part series..."). The best started with a statement about stakes.

**Conversational register:** Up First is "relaxed but not casual." Hosts talk **to** each other about the story, not **at** the listener. The effect is eavesdropping on informed colleagues. Described by one reviewer as "funky elevator music and journalists musing about current affairs in a jazz bar."

### Planet Money: The Named Structure System

Planet Money (weekly, ~25 min) achieves retention through a pre-production methodology that most shows lack:

**The pitch formula:**
> *"My story is about X, which is interesting because of Y."*
If you can't fill in both blanks cleanly, the story doesn't get made.

**Named episode structures (choose before you write):**

| Structure | Description | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **The Thread** | Single character carries the whole story | Personal/human impact stories |
| **Question/Quest** | Question posed upfront, story answers it | Explainers, investigations |
| **Three Reasons** | Listens why X is true | Policy, economics |
| **Through the Rabbit Hole** | Reporter takes listener into a hidden world | Niche subcultures, secret systems |
| **How Did We Get Here** | History lesson for current events | Context-heavy stories |
| **Day in the Game** | Process walk-through, one thing as vehicle | Industry explainers |

**The "AIR" audio checklist:**
- **Actions** — sounds of things happening, not just atmospherics
- **Interactions** — reporter physically engaging with the scene
- **Reactions** — reporter's own surprise/delight/confusion as the listener's surrogate

**The key principle:** Planet Money reporters are *explicitly coached* to model emotional reactions. Their surprise IS the story. This is what creates intimacy — the listener bonds with the reporter's experience of discovery, not just the facts.

**Sign-posting:** Planet Money always tells listeners what it's about to tell them, tells them, then tells them what it just told them. This sounds redundant but it's retention architecture: listeners who miss a detail get a second chance to understand before the episode ends.

## 3. BBC Global News Podcast — International Format

### Show Profile

- **30 minutes**, released **twice daily on weekdays**, once on weekends
- Draws from The Newsroom, BBC's live news show (six broadcasts per day)
- ~33 million downloads/month at peak; 260+ million downloads in 2022
- Primary diaspora audience: UK, India, Nigeria, US, Bangladesh, China, Ethiopia, Australia

### Format Structure

Each 30-minute episode covers **7–8 stories** with clear "light and shade" editorial curation:

| Segment Type | Purpose |
|---|---|
| 2–3 lead geopolitical stories | Hard news authority |
| 1–2 mid-tier regional/niche stories | Breadth and global sweep |
| 1 economics/science/health story | Variety for different listener interests |
| 1 "lighter" story | Emotional reset |

**Editorial principle from host Jackie Leonard:** *"If you have a program which is relentlessly about man's inhumanity to man, catastrophe and disaster, it becomes overwhelming and people glaze over. Human experience is not all grim and relentlessly dark."* Each episode must include a story that reminds listeners the world isn't entirely bleak.

**The "would we cover this if it happened in London?" test:** Before selecting a regional story, editors ask: if this happened in Berlin or New York, how big would this be? This prevents unconscious bias toward Western news values while ensuring global standards.

### How They Handle International/Diaspora Audiences

1. **No assumed shared knowledge.** Jackie Leonard: "There isn't necessarily a shared knowledge on cultural aspects. You may need to spell certain things out." Never assume the listener already knows why something matters.

2. **Correspondents as authenticity.** The podcast's authority comes from having **actual correspondents living in the regions they cover.** They "know how that place or region ticks" — not parachute journalism. People believe their ears.

3. **Consistent release schedule = building into routines.** Listeners tell the BBC they integrate the podcast into their lives at specific times. Predictability is a retention tool independent of content quality.

4. **The BBC's 2025 Africa shift:** In November 2025, BBC World Service announced moving the "Focus on Africa" podcast to Nairobi and co-hosting Newsday from Nairobi and London simultaneously. This signals the direction: authenticity requires presence, not just coverage.

### Two-Voice Format (The Global Story)

In 2023, BBC launched **The Global Story** as a companion deep-dive podcast to the Global News Podcast. By 2025, it was co-hosted by Asma Khalid (Washington DC) and Tristan Redman (London) — two voices, geographically separated, discussing "the intertwined story of America and the world."

Why two voices works here:
- **Geographic split creates structural tension.** One voice represents US perspective, one international. The disagreement is built into the premise.
- **25-minute single-topic format.** Deep dive structure, one story per episode, more like The Daily than Global News Podcast.
- **Gender balance.** Reuters Institute data shows most successful two-host news podcasts use mixed male/female pairings.

## 4. RFI / France Inter — French-Language Diaspora Podcasts

### The Francophone African Diaspora Opportunity

RFI's primary audience pool is **31 million listeners per week**, of which **25 million are in French-speaking Africa.** The diaspora from these regions — across France, Belgium, Canada, and beyond — shares cultural identity with the continent but lives physically outside it.

Key insight: **Audio is the dominant content format in Francophone Africa.** Where smartphones may not support high-bandwidth video, audio podcasts travel on WhatsApp and low-data connections. Format accessibility matters.

### What Works for This Audience

**1. RFI — Grand Reportage**
- 20-minute audio documentaries, broadcast Monday–Thursday at 19:40 UTC
- Reporters speak clearly but use sophisticated vocabulary
- Covers geopolitics, society, conflicts — particularly Francophone Africa, Asia, Americas
- Journalists cover **multiple African accents** naturally; not just Parisian French
- ~27% of listeners are in the US (diaspora), 20% in France, 7.5% in Haiti

**Why it works for diaspora:** Professional journalism delivered in a voice that sounds like home. Not simplified, not patronizing. Respects the intelligence of African and Caribbean listeners.

**2. RFI — Journal en Français Facile**
- Daily 10-minute news in clear, slightly slowed French
- Global news with focus on Francophone regions
- Weekly transcripts available — enables active engagement
- Authentic use case: diaspora members use it to maintain language fluency while staying informed

**3. Hugo Décrypte (France Inter ecosystem / independent)**
- **10-minute daily news digest** — France's most-downloaded podcast
- Founded by 26-year-old Franco-British journalist Hugo Travers
- Targets 15–34 demographic; audience is ideologically diverse (left, right, center)
- **Format philosophy:** "Serious in terms of news and journalistic approach. Of course, the format is different."
- No paywall; active social media; YouTube + audio simultaneously
- The **10-minute format** + **explainer style** has outperformed Le Monde on engagement with young audiences

**Why Hugo Décrypte is instructive for Mole FM:**
- Shows that serious journalism + accessible format + youthful personality = dominant reach with diaspora-adjacent young audiences
- The "accessible not pandering" principle: never assume too little knowledge, never assume too much
- Choosing stories based on audience questions (mental health, underreported African conflicts) creates participatory news

### RFI's Format Authenticity Formula

| Authenticity marker | How RFI achieves it |
|---|---|
| Voice | African and Caribbean accents heard naturally on air, not just as guests |
| Story selection | Africa-first editorial perspective, not an afterthought |
| Correspondents | Reporters physically present in regions they cover |
| Language register | French that sounds like Dakar or Abidjan, not only Paris |
| Topic relevance | Stories that matter to diaspora communities specifically |

**The Afrique Presse model:** Weekly 20-minute podcast hosted by Senegalese journalist Assane Diop. He uses informal language, speaks at natural pace (not artificially slowed), and has a slight African French accent. The authenticity signals "this is by us, for us" rather than a metropolitan broadcast condescending downward.

## 5. Podcast Retention Science

### The Drop-Off Map

Listener retention follows a predictable curve with three high-risk zones:

```
100% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
       ↓ Zone 1: First 5 minutes (HIGHEST RISK)
 75% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                   ↓ Zone 2: Middle third (momentum stall)
 55% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                                      ↓ Zone 3: Final 10 min
 40% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
     0:00           10:00           20:00           30:00
```

**Key thresholds:**
- Lose **>25%** in first 5 minutes → **intro problem**, not content problem
- **20–35% drop** within first 5 minutes is typical for podcasts with weak hooks
- Intro exceeding 90 seconds → **nearly double the drop-off rate** of 30–60 second intros
- Middle third collapse = **momentum stall** (not weak content, but pacing failure)

### Optimal Episode Length by Genre

| Format | Ideal Length | Target Completion Rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Daily News / Briefing | **8–15 min** | **>80%** | Commuter-optimized; length signals efficiency |
| Solo Educational | 15–25 min | >72% | |
| Two-host Weekly | 25–40 min | >70% | Relationship dynamic compensates for length |
| Co-hosted News Deep Dive | 20–30 min | >74% | Spotify 2024 median for this range |
| Interview (Mid) | 30–45 min | >68% | |
| Narrative / Documentary | 25–50 min | >65% | Story arc sustains retention |
| Long-form Interview | 45–75 min | >55% | Requires established audience loyalty |

**The critical number:** Spotify 2024 data — median completion rate for 20–40 minute episodes is **74%**. Above 45 minutes: drops to **58%**. Above 60 minutes: **48%** (unless show has established weekly audience).

**For news specifically:** Daily news briefings 8–15 minutes are the format where **>80% completion is achievable.** This is the NPR Up First insight: listener data shows people WANT short if short means complete.

### The Hook Formula: First 90 Seconds

The goal of the first 90 seconds is not to summarize the episode. It is to **sell the journey**.

**Weak hook (what to avoid):**
> *"Welcome back to the show, I'm [name]. Today we're going to talk about [topic]. We have a great episode for you. Let me also quickly thank our sponsors..."*

**Strong hook (what to do instead):**
> *"Three weeks ago, a container ship disappeared from satellite tracking off the coast of Senegal. Nobody officially reported it. What was on board, and who didn't want us to know — that's what today is about."*

**The hook formula:**
1. **Drop into a specific moment** (not a topic)
2. **State the stakes** (not the subject)
3. **Create a tension that demands resolution** (not an announcement of what you'll cover)
4. **Under 60 seconds** before content begins

**Example scripts for news hooks:**

*Economy story hook:*
> *"The official number is 4.2%. But for the Haitian community in Montréal, the real number feels like something else entirely. Here's why the inflation statistics lie to diaspora families."*

*Politics hook:*
> *"A minister signed a document last Tuesday that almost nobody noticed. By Friday, it could change how 800,000 people in France are classified legally. Let me explain."*

*Human story hook:*
> *"She left Abidjan in 2019 with a master's degree in engineering. She's currently delivering packages in Lyon. This is not an unusual story. That's exactly the problem."*

### Verbal Retention Techniques

**The Cliffhanger / "Coming Up" tease:**
Insert these at natural break points, especially when an ad or transition is coming:

> *"But what the minister said next — that's the part that changes everything. We'll get there right after this."*

> *"There are three things that explain why this keeps happening. We've done two. The third one is the one nobody wants to talk about."*

> *"I'll tell you exactly what was in that document. But first I want to give you the context, because without it, the document makes no sense."*

**The curiosity gap technique:**
State a fact that implies a more interesting fact:
> *"The official cause of death was a heart attack. The coroner's report said something different." → listener cannot stop.*

**Content checkpoints** (for episodes 20+ minutes): At 10, 15, and 20-minute marks, do a brief **reorientation**: "So far we've covered [X]. What we still need to understand is [Y]." This prevents listeners from losing their place and feeling they've missed too much to continue.

**The "pattern interrupt":**
Any abrupt change in pace, register, or sound (a tape clip, a silence, a question directed at the listener) resets attention. Use at least one per 8–10 minutes.

### Music, Sound Design, and Atmosphere in News Podcasts

| Element | Function | Practical Spec |
|---|---|---|
| **Theme music** | Brand identity, emotional priming | 10–20 sec; consistent across all episodes |
| **Music beds** | Emotional emphasis, hold during complex explanation | -20 dB below voice; use sparingly |
| **Stingers / bumpers** | Signal act transitions | 1–3 sec; use same sound each time |
| **Silence** | Emphasis, allow information to land | 1.5–2 sec after major revelation |
| **Field audio / ambient** | Evidence, immersion, place | Never used as decoration; always functions as journalism |
| **UI/transition sounds** | Segment changes | Short swoosh or chime; tech and news podcasts use these |

**Research result:** Podcasts with intentional sound design hold listener attention **34% longer per episode** than talk-only formats (University of Southern California data via Jellypod).

**The "Don't use music as wallpaper" rule:** Wall-to-wall music trains the listener to tune it out. Use silence strategically. A 2-second pause after a dramatic statement hits harder than any music.

## 6. Two-Voice Podcast Dynamics

### What Makes Two Voices Feel Real vs. Robotic

Research on AI-generated two-host podcasts (arXiv 2511.08654) and broadcast coaching reveals the specific markers of authentic vs. synthetic conversation:

**What real two-voice podcasts do:**

| Technique | Why it works |
|---|---|
| **Genuine surprise** | One host actually doesn't know what the other will say. Leave some information off the pre-recording brief. |
| **Micro-interjections** | "m-hm," "right," "wait—" These signal actual listening, not waiting for a turn. |
| **Finishing each other's sentences** | Signals shared understanding; creates intimacy for listeners |
| **Respectful disagreement** | "I see it differently..." maintains intellectual tension. Two hosts who agree on everything = echo chamber. |
| **Address each other by name on air** | Listeners track voices by name, not timbre. This is especially important when voices are similar. |
| **Energy contagion** | In co-hosted formats, energy passes between hosts. When one drops, the other follows. Requires active listening, not passive waiting. |
| **Authentic laughter** | Can't be scripted. The setup can be planned; the laugh cannot. Schedule recording when both hosts are genuinely energized. |

**What sounds robotic:**
- Both hosts reading from the same script
- Consistent agreement ("Right! Exactly! Totally!")
- No interruptions at all (feels edited, not live)
- Host introductions that include credentials ("joining me today is Dr. X, who has a PhD in...")
- Identical vocal energy throughout (no natural rhythm)

**The NotebookLM failure mode:** AI podcasts are described as "synthetic intimacy" — they use all the surface markers (disfluencies, interjections, direct listener address) but lack the embodied, culturally situated knowledge of actual humans. The lesson: **real two-voice authenticity requires hosts who actually have different knowledge, different backgrounds, and genuine opinions that sometimes differ.**

### The "Informed Friend" vs. "News Anchor" Positioning

| Positioning | Characteristics | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **News Anchor** | Authoritative, formal, neutral, professional | Traditional broadcast news |
| **Informed Friend** | Knowledgeable, warm, personal stakes, admits uncertainty, asks real questions | The Daily, The Daily Show |

The **informed friend** positioning achieves higher retention because:
1. It creates parasocial intimacy — listeners feel they're in a personal conversation
2. It permits emotional reaction — friends say "that's outrageous" where anchors say "officials declined to comment"
3. It builds loyalty faster — you return to a friend, not a news feed

**The "expert and curious non-expert" pairing (NPR model):**
One host is the specialist. The other is the intelligent non-expert — the audience surrogate. The surrogate asks the questions the audience is thinking but won't admit to.

This works best when:
- The non-expert genuinely doesn't know the answer (not performing ignorance)
- The non-expert has genuine stakes in the outcome
- The expert is accountable to the non-expert's confusion (not dismissive)

**Warning:** The "surrogate" host can drift into feeling decorative. Both hosts need real roles. If one voice is simply validating the other, that voice is wasting airtime.

### Role Architecture for Two-Host News Podcasts

| Role | Function | Typical characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **The Anchor** | Controls pacing, transitions, ad breaks, CTAs, and brings the episode home | Warm but authoritative; drives the ship |
| **The Analyst / Correspondent** | Goes deeper on substance, provides specific expertise, challenges | Can be more urgent/passionate; less responsible for flow |

**When to assign anchor vs. analyst:** The anchor doesn't have to be the same person every episode. Some shows rotate, which keeps both voices sharp and prevents hierarchy from calcifying. What matters is that the listener feels **organizational clarity** — someone is steering.

**On scripted disagreement:** Pre-plan the **topic** of the disagreement, not the language. Scripted arguments are detectable. If you know "we're going to clash on whether this policy helps or hurts," let the actual words emerge naturally in recording.

**Sample two-voice dynamic scripts for news:**

*Curiosity-gap opening (Host A sets up, Host B pulls tension):*
> **A:** "There's a story that came out of Martinique this week that I can't stop thinking about."
> **B:** "The ferry incident?"
> **A:** "Not the ferry. The thing that happened before the ferry. Which nobody's reporting."
> **B:** "Okay. Walk me through it."

*Informed friend pivot:*
> **A:** "So the parliament voted on Wednesday. And the official interpretation—"
> **B:** "—is that it's good news."
> **A:** "That's what they're saying. But I talked to [person], and she told me something that changes how I read that vote entirely."
> **B:** "Tell me."

*Genuine disagreement (scripted topic, unscripted language):*
> **A:** "I think the government's response was the minimum possible."
> **B:** "I don't know. The minimum possible would have been nothing. They at least—"
> **A:** "That's a very low bar."
> **B:** "Maybe. But given the political constraints—"
> **A:** "The political constraints are the story. That's exactly what I want our listeners to understand."

## 7. Monetization-Friendly Podcast Design

### Where to Place Ads for Maximum Completion + Ad Retention

| Placement | Position | Duration | CPM Rate | Completion of Ad | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Pre-roll** | Start of episode | 15–20 sec | $15–25 | Lower (many skip first 2 min) | Brand awareness only; keep very brief |
| **Mid-roll** | 40–60% into episode | 60–90 sec | **$25–50** | Highest | Direct response, conversions |
| **Post-roll** | After outro | 15–30 sec | $10–15 | Lowest | Secondary sponsors, house ads |

**The mid-roll dominates.** Mid-roll ads convert 2–3x better than pre-roll or post-roll. Listeners who reach the 40% mark are invested. Their attention is monetizable.

**For episodes under 30 minutes:** Single mid-roll only. One well-placed 60-second ad at the 40–60% mark.

**For episodes 20–40 minutes (Mole FM target zone):**
- Optional 15-second pre-roll (brand acknowledgment only, no pitch)
- One mid-roll at 40–50% mark (60–90 seconds)
- Optional post-roll for lower-tier sponsors

**Ad load tolerance rule:** Maximum **4–6 minutes of advertising per hour** of content. Beyond 8 minutes per hour: significant listener skip behavior. Frequency of breaks matters more than total ad time — multiple short breaks feel worse than one longer one.

**Completion rate benchmark:** Above 70% completion = mid-roll ad was almost certainly heard. Between 50–65%: acceptable for awareness. Below 50%: a significant portion of listeners leave before mid-roll even plays.

### How to Read Sponsor Messages So People Don't Skip

**The cardinal rule:** Never say "we're going to take a quick break" or "and now a word from our sponsor." This is labeling the next 60 seconds as skippable. LinkedIn podcast coach Dave Hamilton: *"All you've done is tell them it's time to skip."*

**What to do instead — the personal pivot technique:**

1. **Start with something personal**, not the product name
2. **Don't mention "sponsor" immediately** — earn attention first
3. **Weave the ad into your content flow**

**Example (BAD):**
> *"Before we continue, we want to thank today's sponsor, BrandName. BrandName offers..."*

**Example (GOOD):**
> *"Actually, this story reminded me of something I've been using lately. I've been trying to... [personal story]... which is why when BrandName reached out, I actually paid attention. What they do is..."*

**Host-read ads vs. produced spots:**
- Host-read ads generate **50%+ higher brand recall** than produced spots (academic research: JPRA, 2025)
- Key: read conversationally, not from verbatim script
- Provide hosts with **bullet points and key benefits** — let them find their own words
- Authenticity is the asset; scripted reading undermines it

**The best ad structure (60-second mid-roll):**

```
0:00–0:15   Personal story or relatable hook (no product mention)
0:15–0:40   Product/service description woven through the story
0:40–0:55   Specific offer, code, or URL (mentioned twice)
0:55–1:00   Smooth return to content: "Anyway, back to what we were saying..."
```

**URL/code best practices:**
- Use **simple, memorable URLs** (yourpodcastname.com/sponsor, not complex affiliate links)
- Mention the URL or code **twice**
- Include the link in show notes (audio listeners look these up during or after)

### Completion Rates Are the Real Currency

Advertisers increasingly calculate **Adjusted CPM** = (Published CPM ÷ Completion Rate) × 100

A show with a $40 CPM and 50% completion is less valuable per actual impression than a show with $25 CPM and 85% completion. **A tight, retention-optimized show can command premium rates even at smaller listener counts.**

**Elite threshold:** Completion rate above 75% is "elite" by industry standards. Above 80% is exceptional. Mole FM should target 75%+ as an anchor metric for all format decisions.

## 8. Master Format Template for Mole FM

### Recommended Episode Architecture (20–25 minutes)

This template synthesizes best practices from The Daily, Up First, BBC Global, Hugo Décrypte, and the retention science above.

```
[0:00–0:45]  COLD HOOK
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Drop into a specific moment. Stakes, not topic.
No "welcome" until after the hook lands.
Example: "Something happened in Lomé last week that most 
media missed. By the end of today, you'll understand why 
you should care — even if you've never heard of Lomé."

[0:45–1:30]  BRIEF SHOW ID + EPISODE PROMISE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"Je suis [Host A]. Je suis [Host B]. Vous écoutez Mole FM.
Aujourd'hui: [one-line promise of what they'll walk away with]."
Under 60 seconds. Confirmed thesis statement.

[1:30–6:00]  ACT 1: THE SETUP
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Build context and curiosity. Who are the characters? 
What happened? Why did it happen?
Two-voice: Host B asks the questions the listener is 
already forming. Host A provides grounded context.
Include a piece of field audio or archival tape if available.

[6:00–6:30]  TRANSITION + TEASE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"But here's what we still need to understand — and this 
is the part that surprised me..."
```

### The Informed Friend Test

Before releasing any episode, apply this check: *Would a well-informed friend who just heard this story be able to explain it to their partner at dinner tonight?* If yes, the episode succeeded. If no, the complication (Act 2) wasn't clear enough.

## Quick Reference: The Essential Rules

1. **Hook = stakes, not topic.** Never summarize the episode in the opening. Create a tension that demands resolution.

2. **90 seconds is the drop-off cliff.** Intro music + welcome + who you are must all fit in under 90 seconds before actual content begins.

3. **One story goes deeper than three stories goes wide** (for deep-dive formats). Know which format you're making before you record.

4. **The "informed friend" beats the "news anchor."** Intimacy + expertise + admitted uncertainty = higher retention than formal authority.

5. **Two voices need different knowledge, not just different roles.** If both hosts would give the same answer, one is decorative.

6. **Mid-roll at 40–60% of runtime, never announce it as a "break."** The smoothest transition is a personal pivot.

7. **Sound design is not decoration.** Music beds, stingers, and field audio hold attention 34% longer. Use the same stinger for each transition so listeners learn your audio grammar.

8. **Diaspora audiences need correspondents, not commentators.** Authenticity comes from voices that come from the place, not voices parachuted in to cover it.

9. **Light and shade is editorial strategy, not entertainment.** Every hard-news episode needs one story that restores the listener's faith in humans.

10. **Completion rate is your real product.** Advertisers who understand adjusted CPM pay more for 80% completion at 5,000 listeners than 50% completion at 20,000.
